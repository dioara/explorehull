# Getting Around Hull: Transport and Parking Guide

**Category:** Travel Tips  
**Author:** ExploreHull Editorial Team  
**Reading Time:** 5 minutes

Hull is a compact, flat city that's easy to navigate whether you're on foot, by bike, using public transport, or driving. Understanding your transport options helps you make the most of your visit, getting between attractions efficiently while avoiding common pitfalls. Here's everything you need to know about getting around Hull.

## Walking: The Best Way to See Hull

Hull's city center is highly walkable, with most major attractions within a 20-30 minute walk of each other. The flat terrain makes walking easy, and the compact layout means you can cover significant ground without exhausting yourself.

**Key Walking Routes:**
- Old Town to Marina: 15-minute walk along the waterfront
- City Center to Princes Avenue: 20-minute walk through residential streets
- Marina to The Deep: 10-minute waterfront walk
- Ferens Gallery to Humber Street Gallery: 10-minute walk through Fruit Market

The city center is pedestrian-friendly, with dedicated walking areas and relatively light traffic. Sidewalks are generally well-maintained, and major routes are clearly signposted.

For longer walks, Hull offers several pleasant routes. The waterfront path from the Marina to The Deep provides Humber views and fresh air. The walk from city center to Pearson Park via Princes Avenue takes you through one of Hull's most attractive neighborhoods.

## Cycling: Flat and Bike-Friendly

Hull's flatness makes it ideal for cycling. The city has invested in cycling infrastructure, with dedicated cycle lanes on major routes and bike-friendly policies.

**Bike Hire:** While Hull doesn't have a city-wide bike-share scheme, several shops offer bike rentals. Check locally for current options.

**Cycling Routes:** The city center is navigable by bike, though watch for pedestrian areas where cycling isn't permitted. Quieter residential streets like those in The Avenues provide pleasant cycling routes. The waterfront offers scenic cycling with dedicated paths in places.

**Security:** Use a good lock—bike theft exists in Hull as in any city. Many cafes and attractions have bike racks, and the university campus has secure bike storage.

## Public Transport: Buses

Hull's public transport is primarily bus-based, operated by several companies with East Yorkshire Buses being the largest.

**Coverage:** Bus routes cover the entire city and extend to surrounding areas. Major routes run frequently during the day, with reduced service in evenings and Sundays.

**Fares:** Single fares start around £2-3 depending on distance. Day tickets offer unlimited travel and better value if you're making multiple journeys. Contactless payment is accepted on most buses.

**Key Routes:**
- City Center to University: Multiple routes along Cottingham Road
- City Center to Hessle Road: Regular service
- City Center to East Park: Several routes

**Bus Stations:** Hull Interchange near Ferensway is the main bus station, serving most routes. It's modern, well-signposted, and centrally located.

**Tips:** Buses can be infrequent on some routes, so check timetables. Real-time information is available at major stops and via apps. Drivers are generally helpful if you're unsure which bus to take.

## Taxis and Ride-Sharing

Taxis are readily available in Hull, particularly in the city center and near major attractions.

**Taxi Ranks:** Main ranks are at Hull Paragon Interchange (train station), in the city center near Queen Victoria Square, and at the Marina. Taxis also wait outside popular venues in evenings.

**Booking:** You can hail taxis from ranks or book by phone. Several local taxi companies operate 24/7.

**Ride-Sharing:** Uber operates in Hull, providing an alternative to traditional taxis. Prices are generally competitive, and the app interface is convenient.

**Costs:** Short city center journeys typically cost £5-8. Longer trips (e.g., city center to university) run £8-12. Prices increase in evenings and weekends.

## Driving and Parking

If you're driving to Hull, the city is accessible via the M62 motorway and A63. However, driving within the city center isn't always necessary or advisable.

**Parking:**

**City Center:** Multiple car parks serve the city center. Princes Quay Shopping Centre has a large car park (charges apply). Street parking exists but is limited and often restricted to short stays.

**Marina:** The Marina has parking, though it can fill up on sunny weekends. Some spaces are reserved for residents, so check signage.

**Princes Avenue/The Avenues:** Street parking is available but can be challenging, particularly evenings and weekends. Some streets have resident-only restrictions.

**Costs:** City center car parks typically charge £1-2 per hour, with daily maximums around £8-10. Some offer evening and weekend discounts.

**Park and Ride:** Hull doesn't have a formal park and ride system, but parking at outer locations and taking a bus is possible.

**Traffic:** Hull traffic is generally manageable compared to larger cities, though rush hours (8-9am, 5-6pm) see increased congestion on main routes. The A63 can be particularly busy.

**Tips:** If staying in the city center, consider leaving your car parked and walking or using public transport. Parking at your accommodation and exploring on foot often works better than driving between attractions.

## Train: Arriving and Departing

**Hull Paragon Interchange** is Hull's main train station, serving routes to London, Manchester, Leeds, and other major cities. The station is modern and centrally located, making it easy to start exploring immediately upon arrival.

From the station, you can walk to most city center attractions within 10-15 minutes. Taxis and buses are available directly outside for longer journeys.

## Accessibility

Hull is relatively accessible for those with mobility challenges. The flat terrain helps, and major attractions have wheelchair access. Buses have low floors and ramps. However, some older buildings and areas have limited accessibility, so check in advance if this is a concern.

## The Verdict

Hull's compact size and flat terrain make it easy to navigate. Walking is often the best option for exploring the city center and nearby neighborhoods. Cycling works well for longer distances or those who prefer two wheels. Buses provide coverage across the city, though walking is often quicker for central locations. Driving is possible but not always necessary—consider whether you really need a car for your Hull visit.

The key is matching transport to your needs. Staying in the city center? Walk everywhere. Exploring outer neighborhoods? Use buses or cycle. Visiting attractions spread across the city? Combine walking with occasional bus or taxi rides.

Hull's transport infrastructure isn't perfect, but it's functional and improving. The city's size means you're never far from your destination, and the flat terrain means getting there is rarely a struggle. With a bit of planning and these tips in mind, navigating Hull is straightforward, leaving you free to focus on enjoying what the city offers.

---

**Transport Quick Tips:**
- Walk for city center attractions (most within 20-30 minutes of each other)
- Use buses for outer neighborhoods (£2-3 single fare, day tickets available)
- Cycle if you're comfortable—Hull is flat and increasingly bike-friendly
- Park once and walk rather than driving between attractions
- Hull Paragon Interchange (train station) is centrally located and well-connected
